Heat Treatment Process

Annealing / Normalization

We do annealing process in metals like steel, copper, aluminium and brass to change its mechanical and electrical properties to reduce its hardness and increase its ductility. This reduces the impact of stressful processes on metals such as grinding, machining and prevents distortion during higher temperature heat treatments

For steels, subcritical annealing takes place at 1000°F – 1200°F, so there is no crystal structure change. Intermediate annealing is carried out at 1200°F – 1400°F, so there is some transformation to austenite and full annealing involves completely austenitizing the work at 1500°F – 1700°F.

Parts can be annealed in a vacuum or reducing atmosphere where a bright surface finish is needed. Annealing in air is employed where surface finish is not an important factor and an endothermic/neutral atmosphere may be used during annealing to control decarburisation.

Hardening / Through Hardening

We do this process to achieve high hardness/strength on steel. It consists of austenitising, quenching and tempering, in order to retain a tempered martensite or bainite structure

The neutral hardening is highly beneficial. The heavy loaded parts are given high strength , toughness .and temperature resistance and made lighter and more stiff.

We subject the parts to grinding to lower their roughness and make it acquire machinability. If the part is made of stainless steel ,the corrosion is only available after heat treatment

Tool steels: the desired properties of high hardness, wear resistance, heat resistance and machinability can only be given by hardening.

Carburizing Process

We perform Carburizing, also referred to as Case Hardening, that produces a surface which is resistant to wear, while maintaining toughness and strength of the core. This treatment is applied to low carbon steel parts after machining, as well as high alloy steel bearings, gears, and other components.

Carburizing increases strength and wear resistance. This treatment is applied to low carbon steels after machining.

Strong and very hard-surface parts of intricate and complex shapes can be made of relatively lower cost materials that are readily machined or formed prior to heat treatment.

Nitriding Process

The process of Gas nitriding, which we also specialze in, is a surface hardening process, where nitrogen is added to the surface of steel parts using dissociated ammonia as the source. This develops a very hard case in a component at relatively low temperature, without the need for quenching.

We carry out nitriding at temperatures below the transformation temperature of alloy steels, so that with proper manufacturing techniques, there is little or no distortion as a result of the process. Parts to be nitrided are heat treated to the proper strength level, and final machined. The parts are then exposed to active nitrogen at a carefully controlled temperature, . This temperature is typically below the final tempering temperature of the steel so that nitriding does not affect the base metal mechanical properties. As a result, a very high strength product with extremely good wear resistance can be produced, with little or no dimensional change.

We nitride the components to make them stress relieved prior to final machining so that the only size changes observed are growth of about 0.0005". In some cases, nitrided components are surface nitrided to remove the outermost brittle layer produced by the process, or to bring parts into a tight tolerance.

Tempering Process

We do tempering to improve the characteristics of a metal, especially steel, by heating it to a high temperature, though below the melting point, then cooling it, usually in air. This toughens by lessening brittleness and reduces internal stresses. We vary the temperature depending on the type of steel and designed application; for tool steels, the hardness of which must be retained. The term is also used for hardening by cold-working, as in drawing wire or rolling sheet steel.
